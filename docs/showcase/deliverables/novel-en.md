# The Cartographer of the Drifting Realm

Wren Talmadge weighted her ink before she trusted the wind. It was the first habit her master had drilled into her, twenty years before anyone let her near a real commission: an unweighted pot on an open drafting board was a stain waiting to happen, or worse, a black comet trailing off the gunwale of a wind-skiff at four hundred feet. She set the little lead ring around the base of the pot now, out of nothing but habit, though the only wind in the room came from the window she'd left cracked for the gulls.

They rode the morning thermals off the underside of Corvane in loose, unbothered spirals, close enough that she could hear the creak of their wings against the air. Below them, out of sight from her third-floor window, the guildhall's laundry lines swung between anchor-cables strung island to island, sheets and shirts going stiff in the cold before the sun found the ropes. She had drawn those lines once, for a training exercise, back when she'd thought the hardest part of the craft was getting a straight edge on a curved cable. She knew better now. The room smelled the way it always did in the cold months, of dried vellum and the chalk dust that gathered no matter how often she swept — a smell she'd stopped noticing sometime in her first apprentice year, and only ever caught again on someone else's coat.

She bent back over the board. The commission in front of her was ordinary work — a mooring survey for the outer ring, three islands and a channel, the kind of chart a runner could carry across the city in an afternoon and no one would remark on it either way. Her nib scratched a coastline into permanence, steel on vellum, and she liked the sound of it, the small definite noise of a fact being made. Ink didn't apologize. It went down where you put it and stayed.

Not all of her charts had learned that lesson.

She didn't look at the map-chest at the foot of her table. She rarely did, working; it sat there the way a low piece of furniture sits in any room, present without being seen, and she had trained herself well enough that most days she could go from waking to sleeping without once letting her eyes catch on the brass corners. But she knew where the third roll from the bottom was. She knew, without checking, that it was a third finished — coastline, two islands, the beginning of a wind-rose — and that the rest of the vellum inside it was still the pale, waiting color it had been eleven years ago, the year she'd stopped adding to it and started letting other people call the matter closed, without ever once agreeing.

She capped the pot she wasn't using and reached for a reed pen instead of the steel nib, softer, better for fill-work, and that was when the knock came — three short raps, a guild runner's knock, not the building super's heavier hand.

The boy at her door was maybe twelve, breathing like he'd taken the stairs rather than wait for the lift-basket, an Ashgate ring badge stitched crooked to the breast of his coat. He held out a folded chit sealed with the guild's plain wax stamp, no ribbon, the kind of seal that meant business rather than honor.

"Guildmaster wants you," he said, still catching his breath. "This morning. She said not tomorrow."

Wren took the chit and didn't open it in front of him. She thanked him, gave him the coin from the dish by the door that every chartsmith kept for exactly this, and shut the door again before she broke the wax.

Ashe's hand, unmistakable: three lines, no flourish. *Come up. Bring your good compass.*

She looked, for one unguarded moment, at the map-chest.

Then she put on her coat.

## The Commission

Wren climbed six flights rather than wait for the lift-basket, which was petty of her and she knew it, and arrived at Guildmaster Ashe's door only slightly out of breath.

Ashe didn't look up from the report in front of her. "Sit."

Wren sat. The office smelled of pipe smoke and old rope. Behind Ashe's desk hung the guild's founding tapestry — the ring islands stitched in faded thread by hands two centuries dead, Corvane a single gold knot at the dead center of it, the thread gone the soft brown of things left too long in the sun. Beside it, more recent and far less decorative, hung the guild's working wall-map of the same islands, inked and re-inked a hundred times over, with one wedge left permanently blank where the coastline gave out and the paper simply stopped pretending to know anything.

"Two charts," Ashe said, still not looking up. "Ordell's and Fane's. Both of the Drift's near shelf. Both delivered on time, both signed, both—" she turned the report around so Wren could see it "—in complete disagreement about where the coastline runs on the third island past the shelf-break."

Wren looked. It wasn't a small disagreement. Ordell's chart put a headland where Fane's put open water.

"They didn't survey together?"

"Three weeks apart. Same water." Ashe set the report down and, for the first time, looked at her properly — a long look, assessing, not unkind. "The consortium wants the near shelf charted well enough to moor a grain barge on it by the start of the season. I want a chartsmith who won't come back with a story I have to explain to the harbor office."

"You have chartsmiths."

"I have two who've already tried, and one—" Ashe stopped.

It was a small thing, the stopping. A held breath where a name should have gone, the kind of pause a person only makes around a name they've decided not to say. Wren had heard that particular silence before, from harbor clerks and old guild hands and, twice, from strangers who'd worked out something about her surname a beat too late to unknow it. She let it sit in the room, unfinished, exactly the length of a name.

"—one who went out before them," Ashe said instead, and picked the report back up, and did not put the name into the space she'd left for it.

"Two weeks," she went on, brisk again. "Standard fee, hazard on top. A new skiff from the yard — Ordell's took some damage he hasn't fully explained." A ghost of dry humor there, gone as fast as it came. She slid a folded chit across the desk. "I'm not going to pretend this is an ordinary commission, Talmadge. I wouldn't be able to look at you while I did it."

Wren didn't pick up the chit right away.

"You could give it to someone who hasn't got a reason to want it," she said.

"I could." Ashe didn't blink. "I'd rather give it to the best chartsmith I have, who happens also to have a reason. That isn't the same as giving it to you because of the reason."

It was, a little. They both knew it. Neither said so.

Wren picked up the chit.

"Two weeks," Ashe said again, already turning back to her report, the conversation closed the way a ledger closes — total struck, nothing carried forward that didn't need to be. "Bring me a coastline that holds."

## First Crossing

The wind-skiff took the shelf-break at first light, sail cracked taut, Wren's drafting board strapped low across her knees where the ballast wanted it, ink pots clipped to the gunwale against the pitch.

She began, as she always began, in pencil.

It was practical, mostly. A committed line in ink was a claim she'd have to defend to the next chartsmith who came after her and found the coastline somewhere else. Pencil could be crossed out. Pencil could wait to see if a bearing held. She marked the first channel — a strait the guild's most recent survey placed running clean between two shelf-islands, wide enough for a barge — and then looked up from the chart to check it against the water, the way she always did, out of habit more than doubt.

There was no channel.

The two islands sat closed against each other, seam to seam, moss already grown thick across the join, gray-green and undisturbed, the kind of moss that took a season at least to establish itself on bare rock. She circled the skiff twice, low, close enough to have touched the moss with an oar if she'd had one out. It held under a second look the same as the first. She marked it in pencil — *closed, not open, contra last survey* — cross-hatched the mark lightly so whoever inked this chart next would know not to trust it without checking again, and moved on.

That was the discipline of it, out here. You didn't ask why. You marked what was in front of you, cross-hatched what you weren't sure of, and let the ink wait until a bearing had held across two passes. She'd learned the method from a man who'd learned it from his own master, refined by nobody's theory and everybody's caution, three generations deep.

By midmorning she'd fixed a bearing on a lone island with a dead tree standing black against its northern point — a good landmark, distinctive, the kind of thing a barge captain could pick out at distance and know exactly where he was. She marked it, checked it twice, moved a half-league on to survey the shelf's inner curve.

At dusk, working her way back along the same line to close the day's chart, she found the island again.

Same shape. Same dead tree, black and leafless on the same northern point, unmistakable. Forty degrees further west than the bearing she'd fixed six hours before, as if it had simply walked there while her attention was elsewhere.

She didn't say anything, out loud or to herself. She marked the new bearing in pencil beside the old one, cross-hatched both, and noted the discrepancy in the margin the way she'd note any measurement that didn't reconcile — flatly, for whoever read the chart after her, no comment attached.

A loop of gray gulls kept her company the whole crossing, riding some circuit of their own that never widened and never broke, no matter which way the wind came at them — a closed racetrack traced against gray sky. Real gulls scattered when a gust caught them wrong. These didn't. She watched them longer than the work required, made herself stop, and went back to the board.

Her compass didn't help. This close to the shelf's inner edge, the needle didn't so much swing as drift toward a decision and settle into it without much conviction, slower every hour, as though the whole idea of a fixed direction were something the realm out here had only half agreed to.

It was the chalk that got her, in the end — not the vanished channel, not the walking island, not even the gulls. A cliff face on the shelf's western curve, freshly exposed by some old rockfall, gave off the same flat mineral smell as the practice slates Talis used to chalk bearings onto for her, years before she was old enough to be trusted with a real chart. She'd have known that smell blindfolded. For one full breath she was eleven years old again, chalk dust on her knuckles, being told to find north from a shadow and a guess. Then the wind shifted and took the smell with it, and she was thirty-one, in a skiff, with work to finish before the light went.

The gulls' circuit had a call in it too, she noticed on the last pass — two rising notes and a longer third, over and over, indifferent to her noticing. It sat half a beat from a whistle Talis used to give her on their earliest surveys together, the one that meant *come back now, we're done for the day.* She caught herself waiting for the second half of it, the part that never came from a gull.

Dusk came on fast at the shelf's edge — faster, she thought distantly, than the sun's position by rights should have allowed, though she didn't have the light left to argue with it. She turned the skiff for the ring islands with the day's chart only half fixed in ink, half still in pencil and cross-hatch, waiting to be trusted.

## The Unmade Chart

On the fourth day, or maybe the fifth — she had stopped trusting the light enough to count with any confidence — the shelf gave way to a run of smaller islands she hadn't surveyed since she was a girl, and one of them stopped her cold.

A shingle beach, curved the particular way a held breath curves. A rock at the waterline shaped like a bent knee. A stand of black fig trees behind it, dense enough to swallow the light early.

She knew this cove. She had sat on that exact curve of shingle at nine years old while Talis paced the treeline calling out landmarks for her to find on a chart he'd let her hold herself for the first time, his hand ready but not touching, in case she got it wrong. She had gotten it wrong twice and right the third time, and he had said *good, now do it without looking at me* and walked off to check the water himself, whistling.

Her pencil stopped mid-line. Cold went up the back of her neck and stayed there.

The rock was right. The curve was right. The one thing that wasn't was the treeline — where memory kept a hollow, lightning-split stump standing sentinel over the whole scene, a whole fig tree stood instead, roots thick and old, exactly the age they'd need to be if they'd grown from the wreck of an older, split trunk. The shingle itself was paler than she remembered, a shade off, the kind of thing she'd have put down to her own memory's error if the rest of the cove hadn't matched so precisely it made her teeth ache.

She didn't chart it. Not yet. She took the skiff in low along the treeline instead, past where the commission's route bent back toward the required survey line, telling herself she was only confirming the landmark for the record. A half-mile past the route's edge she was still telling herself that, scanning shadow after shadow between the fig trunks for nothing she could have named if asked.

The wind went first.

It didn't drop so much as stop, all at once, the sail dropping slack across the mast the instant a held cloth is let go. A wind-skiff without wind was a basket on a stick; she had maybe a quarter hour of drift-height before she'd need to put down somewhere, and the somewhere available to her was the cove and nowhere else. Light was going too, fast, faster than she wanted to think about with the sun still technically above the ring in the west. In the uncapped pot at her elbow the ink had started to skin at the edges, a thin dulling film forming across the surface the way it did in real cold, though the air hadn't felt that cold a minute before.

She put the skiff down on the shingle.

For a long moment she didn't do anything at all — didn't chart, didn't search the treeline further, didn't call out, though some old reflex in her chest wanted to. She sat with her hands flat on the drafting board and let the quarter hour tick down toward whatever it was going to be.

Then she reached into the bottom of her satchel, past the folded route-map and the spare nibs, to where Talis's compass had ridden the whole trip without her once taking it out. She hadn't decided to bring it, not consciously, the way you don't decide to bring a scar. Brass, dulled with handling, the needle sticky on one side from a fall he'd taken off a rooftop the year before he made chartsmith. It hadn't pointed true in years.

She climbed out onto the shingle and set it upright in a crack between two stones, the way you'd set a candle where it would catch the wind, except there was no wind left to catch it. She didn't say anything to it. She didn't kneel, or linger, or look back at it once she'd climbed into the skiff and found enough lift in the last thin current of air to get airborne again.

What she took with her instead, before the light went entirely, was a single fixed bearing on the cove, inked this time, no cross-hatch, no hedge — the coastline, the rock, the treeline, rendered as plainly and accurately as any other feature on the chart she owed the guild. Nothing in the lines said what the place had been to her. That wasn't the chart's job.

She flew the rest of the way back to the ring islands in the dark, by feel more than sight, and didn't look down again.

## Coda

Ashe unrolled the finished chart across her desk without ceremony and checked it, line by line, against the guild's older reports — a full minute of silence broken only by the small sound of her finger tracing a coastline. Then she nodded, once. It was the nod she gave finished work that met the standard, no more, no less, and Wren had learned a long time ago not to want more from it.

"This will hold," Ashe said. It was as close to praise as the guildmaster came before witnesses, and there were none, which meant it wasn't performance either.

Behind her, the wedge of blank paper on the working wall-map had shrunk — not closed, not filled in with anything she hadn't actually surveyed, but smaller than it had been two weeks before, the coastline of the near shelf pushing the unknown back toward the Drift's deeper reaches where it belonged. The deep interior past the cove stayed exactly as blank as Wren had left it: unornamented, unexplained, a plain white space where a lesser chartsmith might have been tempted to sketch something in rather than admit the survey ended there. Ashe glanced at the blank space, said nothing about it, and countersigned the chit.

The fee was mentioned once, briskly, in the same offhand tone as any ordinary transaction, and then Wren was walking back down the guildhall stairs into the last low light of the day.

Her own workshop held the particular quiet of evening — gulls settling for the night along the anchor-cables outside her window, the laundry lines gone still. She set her satchel down and, before she did anything else, before she ate or lit the second lamp, she crouched at the map-chest and drew out the roll from the third place from the bottom.

Coastline in her hand, steadier now than it had been at twenty. Two islands and the beginning of a wind-rose in a hand slightly grayer with age, stopped mid-stroke eleven years before. She unrolled it flat across the drafting board, weighted the corners, and uncapped the ink she'd carried the whole survey without using for this.

One line. The width of a cove, no more, matched to the bearing she'd taken on the shingle, in a hand as careful as the rest of the old chart, inventing nothing past what she had actually seen.

Then she capped the ink, and did not roll the chart back up with the others. She set it, still open, on top of the rest when she closed the chest lid, and in the last of the window-light a fine drift of chalk dust rose off the closing lid and hung there, turning slowly, before it settled.
